What is Ethics?

Definition

Ethics is the study of what is right or wrong, good or bad, and acceptable or unacceptable in human behaviour. It is a set of moral principles that guides how we should behave.

💡

Ethics means knowing and choosing what is
right, fair, honest and responsible.

In the workplace

Ethics guides employees and employers to behave responsibly, honestly, fairly and professionally when dealing with customers, colleagues, managers, suppliers, competitors and society.

Example: An employee finds confidential salary information on a shared computer. Even though they can open the file, the ethical action is not to read, copy or share it — out of respect for privacy and confidentiality.

Questions ethics helps us ask

  • Is this action right or wrong?
  • Is this action fair to everyone affected?
  • Is this action honest — is anyone being misled?
  • Who will be affected by this decision?
  • Can I justify and explain why this decision is acceptable?

Why Ethics Matters in Business

A business cannot survive by focusing on profit alone. It must also build trust and act responsibly, because every decision affects people, reputation and long-term success.

🤝
Builds trustCustomers trust businesses that are honest and responsible.e.g. a company clearly states its product ingredients
🛡️
Protects reputationEthical behaviour helps a company keep a good name.e.g. a company admits mistakes and solves complaints
🧭
Guides decision-makingEthics helps managers choose the right action.e.g. a manager refuses to accept a bribe from a supplier
🚫
Prevents misconductReduces cheating, fraud and abuse of power.e.g. employees follow the rules on claims and expenses
🌱
Improves workplace cultureCreates respect and fairness among staff.e.g. colleagues treat each other professionally
📈
Supports long-term successEthical companies keep loyal customers and employees.e.g. customers keep buying from a trusted brand

Business Ethics
💼

Business ethics = moral principles and standards that guide behaviour in business activities — doing the right thing even when no one is watching.

It applies to how a business treats its customers, employees, competitors, suppliers, community and environment. It is not only about following rules.

Examples of business ethics

SituationEthical behaviour
Selling productsGive accurate information to customers
AdvertisingDo not make false claims
Customer serviceHandle complaints politely and fairly
PricingAvoid cheating or hidden charges
Employee treatmentTreat employees equally and respectfully
CompetitionDo not spread false information about rivals
Company resourcesDo not misuse office property for personal gain
🧴

A skincare company knows its product may cause irritation for some users. Hiding this may boost short-term sales — but it is unethical because customers can be harmed or misled.

4
Organizational Ethics

Organizational ethics refers to the ethical values, rules, practices and culture inside an organisation — how it expects its members to behave.

Where you can see it

AreaExample
Company policyAnti-bribery policy
Leadership behaviourManagers act honestly and fairly
Workplace cultureEmployees respect one another
Decision-makingThe company considers customer safety, not only profit
Code of conductRules on confidentiality, conflict of interest & conduct
🏢
Business vs Organizational Ethics
tap to compare
▾
Business Ethics

Ethical behaviour in business activities — involving customers, competitors, suppliers and society.

e.g. honest advertising

Organizational Ethics

Ethical values & behaviour inside the organisation — involving employees, managers and internal culture.

e.g. a fair promotion policy

⚠️ A business may say it values honesty — but if managers encourage staff to hide customer complaints, it does not actually practise strong organizational ethics.

Compare: Ethics vs the Rest

Morality, etiquette, law and religion are all related to ethics — but they are not the same thing. Tap each card to open the comparison.

💭
Ethics vs Morality
personal belief vs formal standards
▾
Morality

Personal belief about right and wrong. Differs from person to person. Shaped by culture, religion, family & upbringing.

"I believe lying is wrong"

Ethics

Formal principles or standards of right conduct, used in professional or organizational settings. Shaped by codes & policies.

"Employees must not falsify records"

🎁 A person may believe giving a manager a gift is a normal sign of respect (morality). But in business it can become an ethical issue if the gift influences a decision.
🤝
Ethics vs Etiquette
right & wrong vs manners
▾
Ethics

Focuses on right and wrong. Usually more serious. Breaking it can harm people or the organisation.

e.g. stealing company money

Etiquette

Accepted manners and proper behaviour. Social / workplace expectations. Breaking it causes discomfort or a poor impression.

e.g. interrupting in a meeting

⏰ Coming late to a meeting without informing anyone is poor etiquette. But falsifying attendance records is an ethical issue — it involves dishonesty.
⚖️
Ethics vs Law
what we SHOULD do vs what we MUST do
▾
Law

Formal rules created and enforced by government. Written. Punishment may be fines, court action or imprisonment.

Tells us what we MUST do

Ethics

About what is right, fair and responsible. Often higher than legal compliance. Some legal acts are still unethical.

Tells us what we SHOULD do

🔑 A business should not only ask "Is this legal?" — it should also ask "Is this right and fair?"  See the interactive cases below ⬇️

🎩 Why business etiquette matters

Etiquette is the accepted manners and behaviour in a setting. Good etiquette creates a respectful, comfortable working environment.

TypeExample
CommunicationUse polite language in email and conversation
MeetingsBe punctual and prepared
PhoneSpeak clearly and professionally
CustomerListen to complaints respectfully
DressDress according to workplace expectations
DigitalAvoid sending rude or careless messages online

It matters because it: creates a good first impression, shows respect, reduces conflict, builds confidence, and supports the company image.

Professional Codes

Professional codes are written guidelines explaining how people in a profession or organisation should behave. They set the standard of expected conduct.

Purpose of professional codes

  • Guide behaviour — help employees know what is acceptable
  • Set standards — explain expected professional conduct
  • Prevent misconduct — reduce unethical actions
  • Protect stakeholders — ensure fair treatment for all
  • Support decisions — help handle difficult situations
  • Build trust — show the organisation values ethical conduct

Common elements

ElementExample
HonestyDo not falsify records
IntegrityDo the right thing even under pressure
ConfidentialityDo not share customer data
FairnessAvoid favouritism
RespectDo not insult colleagues
ResponsibilityAdmit mistakes and correct them
No conflict of interestDo not pick a supplier just because they are a relative
ComplianceFollow safety and anti-bribery rules
🎁

A purchasing officer is offered an expensive gift by a supplier before the selection decision. Based on professional ethics, the officer should reject the gift or report it — accepting it could create a conflict of interest.

7
Ethics & Religion

Religion can influence moral values and ethical behaviour. Many religions teach honesty, kindness, fairness, responsibility, respect and compassion.

MoralityReligion
Beliefs about right and wrongA belief system that may guide moral behaviour
Influenced by family, society, culture & religionOften provides teachings about good conduct
May exist with or without religionOften shapes a person's values & responsibilities
🕊️

In business, employees should respect different beliefs. Ethical behaviour should promote fairness, respect, responsibility and professionalism for everyone.

One person may refuse to cheat customers because of religious teaching; another because of personal values or professional ethics. Both lead to ethical behaviour.

Ethics + Etiquette + Professionalism

These three are connected. A professional employee should not only know how to do the job — they should also know how to behave properly.

ConceptFocusExample
EthicsRight & wrong conductDo not misuse company funds
EtiquetteProper manners & behaviourGreet customers politely
ProfessionalismResponsible, appropriate conductBe punctual, respectful & accountable
🌟

Ethics is doing the right thing.
Etiquette is behaving properly.
Professionalism is showing both at work.

Case Study: The Customer Complaint
A customer complains that a product is defective. The staff knows that the company made a mistake during packaging — but the manager tells the staff to say that the customer probably damaged the item.

What are the ethical issues here, and what should the company actually do?

⚠️ Ethical issues in the case

IssueExplanation
HonestyThe staff is asked to hide the truth
ResponsibilityThe company should own its mistake
Customer rightsThe customer deserves fair treatment
Professional ethicsStaff should act on company values & ethical standards
ReputationIf the truth comes out, the company loses trust
✅

The right action: investigate the complaint, admit the mistake if true, apologise professionally, and offer a suitable solution — replacement, refund or repair.

Key Takeaways
✔
Ethics guides people to make responsible, fair and honest decisions in business.
✔
Business ethics covers relationships with customers, employees, suppliers, competitors & society; organizational ethics focuses on values & culture inside the workplace.
